MLB Project

In my Data Science course, my team of three students had to analyze a dataset that we found interesting. We decided to focus on the MLB because of our interest in sports and sabermetrics after reading "Moneyball." Together, we were responsible for coming up with three separate questions that related to the salary of a professional pitcher. Because of my prior experience as a PM, I took on the responsibility of getting our team organized by maintaing a Kanban Board to keep track of our distribution of work and progress to ensure that all tasks were completed by the end of each milestone. Furthermore, I was able to identify sources containing data from the 2021 MLB season and combine all of them to create a "master" data frame in which we can perform our analysis on. This was an exciting project to work on because of the analysis techniques I've learned from in this class. My previous work would just require me to clean the data and create visualizations in which I drew conclusions from. But here, we have learned about machine learning techniques such as linear and logistical regression. So, I was able to fit a logistic regression model using statsmodels that had a 82.65% accuracy rate on whether a given pitcher would make above or below the salary median. Additionally, I was able to implement a random walk optimization code that found a best performing team given a custom metric and constraint of salary. Doing this project made me realize why ML is such a trending field as there is so much predictive information that can be generated from past data. I think these analytics techniques are super intriguing and I am interested in learning more as well as taking more projects where I can apply these skills.



Watercooler Project

This was a project I helped a Cornell Policy Analysis Management PhD Candidate on during the Fall 2021 semester. The goal here was to understand more about how involuntary layoff events affected different parties. More specifically, how do the laid off workers express their sentiments online or what were some financial factors that these publicly traded companies were facing at the time. To help give some more context to this problem, I was responsible for the following technical tasks. First, I had to identify possible sources to extract data from. After doing some research and evaluating my options, I decided to choose Ravenpack because this was the most extensive and structured. So, I wrote code in Python that made API calls to their servers which extracted data from digital newspapers. The second part was after pulling the data and having previous scraped data, I needed to combine them to prepare them for analysis. Having learned how to use dplyr in my PAM course, I used that package to clean over 50,000 rows of data (more than any amount I have done so far)! Lastly, I used ggplot to do some exploratory plots in order to idenitfy possible trends and correlations and presented them to the principal investigator.



Trifecta of Social Justice, Engineering, and Communication

Being an engineer, I realize that we have great power in our hands. A civil engineer has design choices to make over infrastructure which millions of people depend on. A chemical engineer can develop medicine, but they need to run tests to ensure it is safe for human consumption. However, this made me think how software engineers and computer scientists are responsible for creating products that impact people. Personally, I believe that it is essential for individuals use their talents for good, which is why I decided to do a deep dive into the code of ethics for some companies to see how they are protecting their users as they scale. Additionally, I was curious to learn how their day to day operations would tie in to the mission statements that they had before the "tech boom." From this research project, I learned that engineering is anything but "objective." There is no such thing as the right way to do things as we are faced with tradeoffs every day. In fact many design patterns we see can be judgement calls influenced by previous experiences. We also have the responsiblity to think about how accessible our product is as software engineers should constantly be reminded about inclusivity rather than finding a quick patch to a problem. This made me realize that this indeed is a tough challenge to solve as the industry expands, but the integrity of "doing the right thing" is a trait that industry leaders can't lose site over.



BrightInsight

BrightInsight provides the leading global regulated digital health platform for biopharma and medtech. Our medical-grade Internet of Things (IoT) platform is built under a Quality Management System to support and optimize regulated drugs, devices and software through integrated data and actionable insights to enable customers to drive increased patient adherence and engagement.

In summer of 2021, I worked as a Program Management Intern at a startup called BrightInsight. I wanted to explore a career path as a PM because from my previous experience at Cornell, I've enjoyed planning the roadmaps for projects. During this internship, I was able to lead the Data Engineering Team which consisted of 17 engineers as their Scrum Master by leading daily sprint standups to document project status, mitigate risk, and resolve dependencies all on a Jira Board! When not in meetings, I was also responsible for creating and maintaing project timelines for two microservices which required talking to various stakeholders in order to understand scope and specifications as well as delivering projects to our customers on time. Lastly, because of my engineering background, my manager gave a more analytical project as part of the PMO's key performance indicator to improve the time that our code can be shipped out. To do this, I had to write formulas within EazyBI and create visualizations using Jellyfish to present to senior management by showing how the team spends a long time on debugging during the sprint. Therefore, this can be attributed to poor code and design from the past which causes many dependencies or during the development process, the team isn't doing a strong enough job of getting tasks organized. It was quite interesting to me because this shows how businesses are increasingly relying on the use of data to drive decisions. From this internship, I was able to learn more about how Scrum and Agile processes are used in a real world setting. However, the most important thing I took away was that my interest is geared towards analytics and being able to clean up data to create visualizations and tell stories to relevant stakeholders to help improve processes.


Blackjack - CS3110 Final Project

My partner and I developed a game of Blackjack from scratch in OCaml for the final project in our functional programming course. We chose to make this game because we wanted to create something that helped us pass time during the pandemic. This project was a valuable experience, not only because did it help me learn OCaml better, but it allowed me to work through the full Software Development Lifecycle. We used a Trello Board to help us keep track of tasks that had to be done, which helped us immensely during the planning stage since it enabled us to develop the game iteratively by thinking about the core functionalities. After implementing and testing it, we had to structure our code to ensure that it can be incorporated into later parts seamlessly. Upon completition of our game, we were able to create a Blackjack engine that could be played in one's terminal using a REPL format. Furthermore, our game enabled multiple levels of a CPU multi-player as well as the ability to bet. When we were making this game, this was the longest program I have written and I had some takeaways from this experience. First, to ensure that we are making programs of value, it is important to talk to stakeholders so we can get their feedback and improve our code. Second, writing logical/readable code and having comments when working with a team will greatly speed up the debugging process by helping others understand. Lastly, don't underestimate the planning stage as doing a good job will simplify control flow leading to more straight forward code which is what we should strive for.

Housing Inequality - PAM2070 Policy Brief

I took this course because I was curious about what a career would be like as a data scientist. Not having heard much of Policy Analysis Management before, I was intrigued to see how this field would overlap with a more technical discipline. Here, I was first introduced to R and the tidyverse package. For our final project, we had to write a policy brief to address a policy problem that was discussed in the course. I chose to write about housing inequality in Nassau County where I am from. To lobby the use of housing vouchers to increase the intergenerational mobility for children, I cleaned five datasets totaling over 6000 rows and used ggplot to create visualizations. These visualizations helped to contextualize the problem by indicating where Nassau County stood on a variety of factors and which locations within this county needed the most support in order to deter their children from unlawful activities.
